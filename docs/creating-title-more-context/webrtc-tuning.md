# RemoteDesk Desktop - WebRTC Performance Tuning Guide\n\nThis guide provides strategies and best practices for optimizing WebRTC performance in the RemoteDesk desktop application.\n\n## 1. Codec Selection\n\nThe choice of video and audio codecs significantly impacts performance and quality. Different codecs have different trade-offs between quality, latency, and CPU usage.\n\n**Video Codecs:**\n\nH.264 is widely supported and offers good quality-to-CPU-usage ratio. VP8 and VP9 are open-source alternatives with varying levels of hardware acceleration support. VP9 offers better compression but may require more CPU resources.\n\n**Audio Codecs:**\n\nOpus is the recommended audio codec for RemoteDesk due to its superior quality at low bitrates and adaptive bitrate capabilities. PCMU and PCMA are legacy codecs with lower quality.\n\n## 2. Frame Rate and Resolution\n\nAdjusting frame rate and resolution based on network conditions and user preferences can optimize performance.\n\n**Frame Rate:**\n\nA frame rate of 30 FPS is suitable for most use cases. For high-motion content or gaming, 60 FPS might be preferred, but it requires more bandwidth and CPU. For static content or bandwidth-constrained scenarios, 15 FPS or lower might be acceptable.\n\n**Resolution:**\n\nCommon resolutions include 720p (1280x720), 1080p (1920x1080), and 1440p (2560x1440). Higher resolutions provide better clarity but require more bandwidth and CPU resources. Consider the user's monitor resolution and network bandwidth when selecting.\n\n## 3. Bitrate Control\n\nWebRTC automatically adjusts bitrate based on network conditions. However, you can set preferred bitrate ranges to influence this behavior.\n\n```typescript\nconst rtcConfig = {\n  videoBitrate: {\n    min: 500,  // Minimum bitrate in kbps\n    max: 5000, // Maximum bitrate in kbps\n  },\n  audioBitrate: {\n    min: 16,   // Minimum bitrate in kbps\n    max: 128,  // Maximum bitrate in kbps\n  },\n};\n```\n\n## 4. ICE Candidate Filtering\n\nICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment) candidates determine the network paths available for the peer connection. Filtering candidates can reduce connection establishment time and improve performance.\n\n**ICE Candidate Types:**\n\nHost candidates are direct connections from your local machine. Server reflexive candidates are obtained from STUN servers and represent your public IP. Peer reflexive candidates are discovered during the connection process. Relay candidates are obtained from TURN servers and are used when direct connections are not possible.\n\nPrioritize host and server reflexive candidates for lower latency. Use relay candidates as a fallback.\n\n## 5. Hardware Acceleration\n\nLeveraging hardware acceleration for video encoding and decoding can significantly reduce CPU usage. Ensure your graphics hardware and drivers support hardware acceleration for your chosen codec.\n\n## 6. Monitoring and Diagnostics\n\nRegularly monitor WebRTC statistics to identify and address performance issues early. Use the `webrtcStatsAnalyzer.ts` utility to analyze connection quality and identify problems.\n\nCommon issues include:\n\n- **Low Frame Rate:** Indicates CPU or bandwidth constraints. Consider reducing resolution or frame rate.\n- **High Packet Loss:** Indicates network issues. Consider using a TURN server or reducing bitrate.\n- **High RTT:** Indicates network latency. This is harder to address but might improve with a closer TURN server.\n\nBy following these guidelines, you can optimize WebRTC performance for a smooth and responsive remote desktop experience.\n"
